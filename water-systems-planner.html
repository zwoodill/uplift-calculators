Method & references — what the two bases mean
Uo and the zone check — HUD 24 CFR §3280.506. Uo is the whole-home overall coefficient of heat transmission: total envelope conductance UA (Btu/hr·°F) divided by total envelope area (walls + roof + floor, windows & door included), evaluated at a 70 °F indoor design temperature, excluding infiltration and ventilation. The rule caps it by climate zone — Zone 1 ≤ 0.116, Zone 2 ≤ 0.096, Zone 3 ≤ 0.079 Btu/(hr·ft²·°F) — with the zone set by the map in Figure 1 to §3280.506(a) (roughly: Z1 = deep South, Z2 = mid-country, Z3 = northern tier). §3280.506(c) separately requires cavities to be insulated so heat transmission is uniform — scattered bare spots don't average away. §3280.506 (Cornell) · Subpart F (eCFR)
“Design” basis — ASHRAE effective-R (our best physics estimate). ASHRAE is the engineering society whose Handbook of Fundamentals and Standard 90.1 define how assemblies are actually rated. For steel framing, simple parallel-path math fails badly — steel conducts ~400× better than wood, so each member drains heat from a band wider than its own face (the Handbook's “zone method” models this). 90.1 Appendix A therefore publishes measured/modelled effective R-values: e.g. R-13 batt between 3.5″ steel studs @16″ o.c. is worth only R-6.0 — ~46% of nominal. This tab uses that table directly in “studs o.c.” mode, and in member-takeoff mode generalizes it: your schedule's steel face fraction (count × length × face width ÷ area, × a small weld/plate allowance K) is looked up on the same table by steel fraction — its 16″/24″ o.c. columns correspond to ≈10.2% / 6.8% steel — so diagonals and truss members are handled by their actual steel, and the table's built-in lateral-spread (zone) physics is preserved. Continuous board inboard of the frame is unbridged and counts at full R. Effective-R table (UpCodes / IECC A103.3.6.2) · CFSEI cold-formed-steel thermal design guide (PDF)
“HUD certificate” basis — §3280.508 + §3280.509 (the regulatory calculation). The Uo that goes on the heat-loss certificate must follow the 1997 ASHRAE Handbook of Fundamentals procedures and HUD's reference document PNL-8006. Where you don't have specific test data, §3280.509 prescribes defaults: framing counts as 15% of wall area (10% of roof/floor) in a parallel path — that is what this basis switches to — plus rules the later tabs will pick up: infiltration = 0.7·T·perimeter (§3280.509(a)); compressed insulation derated by table (§3280.509(c)); duct rules (d–f). §3280.508 additionally requires framing and any “thermal shorts” exceeding 1% of surface area (in aggregate) to be explicitly counted — for a welded truss frame that is exactly what the member takeoff documents — and window U-values from NFRC 100 / AAMA 1503 testing (or the higher ASHRAE default if unrated). Deliverables downstream: the §3280.510 heat-loss certificate (Uo zone + lowest outdoor temp the heater holds 70 °F) and the §3280.511 comfort-cooling certificate. §3280.508 · §3280.509 · §3280.510 · §3280.511
Which basis when? Use Design to make engineering trade-offs (it's the closest to what the wall will really do). Use HUD certificate to preview the number a DAPIA will check against the §3280.506 cap — with a documented member takeoff you may argue actual framing data in place of the 15%/10% defaults; that's a DAPIA conversation.

Method & references
Load structure (from Charlie's model, corrected per the reconciliation memo). Steady-state conduction Q = A·ΔT / R summed over walls, roof, floor, windows and the door (the memo's dropped-door-term bug is fixed here), on the effective per-surface R from the Envelope tab — steel bridging included on both bases, so there is no fictional uniform R-19 anywhere in this chain. Loads run 24 h; the HVAC cycles but the load is continuous.
Infiltration — the biggest basis difference. Design basis: sensible air-leakage 1.08 × CFM × ΔT with CFM = ACH × interior volume ÷ 60 — defensible only with a blower-door target. HUD certificate basis: the §3280.509(a) absent-data formula 0.7 × T × perimeter (T = 70 °F − heating-cert outdoor temp) — for this unit ~5× the 0.5-ACH number, and it dominates the certified heating load. Cooling infiltration uses the ACH method on both bases (the perimeter formula is a heating-certificate rule). §3280.509
Sol-air & window solar (cooling). Sun makes the roof act hotter than air; the 24-h-average uplift by roof colour (white +12 °F … black +36 °F, from Charlie's Ref table) is added to the outdoor temp for the roof (full) and walls (half). Window solar gain = SHGC × average daytime flux on the glass × glazing area. This is a steady-state approximation, not the ASHRAE Ch. 22 CLTD procedure the §3280.511 comfort-cooling certificate formally names — the certificate panel (P5) will carry that caveat.
Internal gains & the two heating numbers. People count 117 W each total (cooling) / 70 W sensible (heating credit, ASHRAE seated); plug gains come live from the Energy tab's loads table (HVAC row excluded — that's the machine, not a gain). The certified design load takes no gain credit — you can't certify furnace capacity assuming occupants are home (§3280.510). The expected operating load (gains credited) is the number for energy/runtime estimates. Latent adder ≈ 30% of sensible in humid climates. Steady-state throughout — no thermal mass; a design-trade tool, not Manual J. §3280.510 · §3280.511
